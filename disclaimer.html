3. Data Source Limitations

All data used in this project is collected from publicly available posts on online community forums and social media platforms via publicly accessible interfaces. The specific platforms, tools, and technical methods used to collect data are not disclosed.

3.1 Sample Representativeness

  • Social media and online forum users do not represent the general population of any city. These platforms typically skew toward younger, more tech-savvy, and more vocal demographics.
  • Participation in city-based online communities is voluntary and self-selected, which introduces significant selection bias.
  • Residents who do not use social media or online forums — including older adults, non-English speakers, and those without internet access — are not represented in the data.
  • The online communities analysed may attract residents with stronger opinions or grievances, which may not reflect the views of the broader population.

3.2 Sentiment and Activity Signals

  • The digital signals used to measure positive sentiment are proprietary proxies only. They do not directly or definitively measure happiness, life satisfaction, mental health, or overall wellbeing.
  • The absence of positive sentiment signals does not indicate unhappiness. People communicate positively in many ways that may not be captured by this methodology.
  • Activity-related signals reflect social activeness only and do not measure community cohesion, social capital, safety, or quality of social life.
  • Communication styles and online behaviour vary across age groups, cultural backgrounds, and communities, which may affect comparability between cities.
  • The proprietary methodology behind the indexes is not fully disclosed. Results should be interpreted with this in mind.

3.3 Volume and Timing

  • Each data collection run analyses a snapshot of recent posts at a given point in time. Results may vary significantly between runs due to current events, seasonal patterns, or trending topics.
  • Online communities vary considerably in size and activity level. Smaller communities may produce less statistically reliable results.
  • Data is not weighted or adjusted for community size, city population, or demographic differences between cities.

3.4 No Data on Private Communications

  • This project only analyses publicly visible online posts. Private messages, closed groups, and offline communications are not included.
  • The data does not capture the full range of digital or in-person communication by residents of any city.

4. No Defamatory Intent

This project does not intend to defame, disparage, harm, or negatively characterise any city, municipality, community, resident group, or individual. Any ranking or comparison of cities is purely exploratory and is based on publicly available social media data. Lower scores on any index do not imply that residents of a city are less happy, less social, or of lower character in any way.

Cities with lower scores may simply have smaller or less active online communities, different communication cultures, or different demographic profiles — none of which reflect on the actual quality of life or happiness of residents.

8. Suggested Use

This project is best used as one small, informal reference point among many when thinking about community wellbeing. It is most appropriately used:

  • As a conversation starter about how digital sentiment might reflect community mood
  • As a supplementary, informal data point alongside official wellbeing surveys and government data
  • As a longitudinal trend indicator — changes over time may be more meaningful than absolute values

It should not be used as a standalone measure of city happiness, a basis for policy decisions, real estate investment decisions, or any other consequential judgment about a city or its residents.
